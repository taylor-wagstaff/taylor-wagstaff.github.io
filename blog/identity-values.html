Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people.

The principles have the possibility to re-orientate how an individual might change the process of how we learn. It has the capacity to reshape and rewire important neural pathways of the brain through cognitive activity, even the power of imagination without its physical dimension, such as learning to play an instrument through imagination only still can produce learning outcomes as it would its physical. Neuroplasticity benefits people because it allows an individual to overcome their own self-limiting beliefs such as “i'm not good at math” because such a belief actually creates neural pathways and reshapes the brain towards this goal.

Discuss how you might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit.

How I might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity is to take the idea of overcoming self-limiting beliefs and applying that to my mode of learning. In instances where I think that coding is for super intelligent math nerds, I know now that any thoughts like this are designed to impede learning. The principle of repetition I very much engage in and believe in its developmental potential. I have learned many skills using this method, such as playing guitar or painting, which has involved many hours over years to reach the point at which I am now. I will apply the same logic to coding as would any other thing I wish to learn and be good at.

What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

Repetition and overcoming self-limiting beliefs, as stated above, are two ways I could increase my neuroplasticity. I like the idea of imagination being integrated into the learning process. I have been attempting to remember javaScript code through imaginative associations with those functions. In a way this is attempting to remember code by creating new neural connections in the brain to help both with memory and their use cases. For instance, a variable to me is like a mini usb, able to store information and be plugged in at will.

Discuss what Growth Mindset is and why it is relevant.

Growth mindset is the opposite of a fixed mindset. How they are different is that in a growth mindset the individual believes that they are capable of learning even if problems may at first appear too complex or unmanageable. The attitude of a growth mindset would believe that such challenges are naturally part of the learning process whereas a fixed mindset would believe that it is above them or that they do not have the ability to complete difficult problems.

The main integration of the growth mindset is the overcoming of self-limiting beliefs. And when I hit a wall to a problem which seems beyond me, is to recognise that it's all part of the learning process. The idea is to not freak out and embrace the challenge that presents to me in the now. Even if the answer is wrong, as long as I have given it its best shot is all that matters.

Emotional intelligence

What is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional Intelligence is an individual's awareness of their own emotional feelings. This awareness is characterized by the ability for the individual to pay attention to, and respond to, their own feelings in a way that is productive rather than unproductive or dehumanizing. Emotional Intelligence is predominantly shaped by our upbringing, especially in how we both received and gave love in our relationships with our parents. Our childhood and into our adult lives can produce emotional habits and responses which may be detrimental to our emotional well being. Emotional intelligence is the human expression of how we can manage these habits, or even repair and change them into a way of being that is one of lightness.

How is it different to IQ?

IQ, which is short for intelligence quotient, is described as the capacity for an individual to problem solve, use logic in interesting ways, grasp intelligent ideas and communicate them to others. In a sense, IQ is the expression of an individual's intelligence exerted through the world and emotional intelligence is the individual's awareness of their own emotions and their ability to regulate them.

What is Enneagram?

According to the Enneagram, each of the nine personality types is defined by a particular core belief about how the world works. This core belief drives your deepest motivations and fears — and fundamentally shapes a person's worldview and the perspective through which they see the world and the people around them. Our core beliefs are not necessarily incorrect, but they can be limiting and operate as “blinders” for people. Understanding our Enneagram type and how it colors our perceptions can help us to broaden our perspective and approach situations more effectively. Understanding a person's Enneagram type helps us to see why they behave the way they do. Each Enneagram type has a set of core beliefs that will consistently motivate them to take particular actions and guide them to make certain decisions. Behavior that may seem confusing or contradictory can often be explained when we understand a person's Enneagram type. The Enneagram also helps us understand how people react to stress. By describing how each Enneatype adapts and responds to both stressful and supportive situations, the Enneagram shows opportunities for personal development and provides a foundation for the understanding of others.

Type Three in Brief:

Threes are self-assured, attractive, and charming. Ambitious, competent, and energetic, they can also be status-conscious and highly driven for advancement. They are diplomatic and poised, but can also be overly concerned with their image and what others think of them. They typically have problems with workaholism and competitiveness. At their Best: self-accepting, authentic, everything they seem to be—role models who inspire others.

  • Basic Fear: Of being worthless
  • Basic Desire: To feel valuable and worthwhile
  • Enneagram Three with a Two-Wing: "The Charmer"
  • Enneagram Three with a Four-Wing: "The Professional"

Key Motivations:

Want to be affirmed, to distinguish themselves from others, to have attention, to be admired, and to impress others.

Everyone needs attention, encouragement, and the affirmation of their value in order to thrive, and Threes are the type which most exemplifies this universal human need. Threes want success not so much for the things that success will buy (like Sevens), or for the power and feeling of independence that it will bring (like Eights). They want success because they are afraid of disappearing into a chasm of emptiness and worthlessness: without the increased attention and feeling of accomplishment which success usually brings, Threes fear that they are nobody and have no value.

The problem is that, in the headlong rush to achieve whatever they believe will make them more valuable, Threes can become so alienated from themselves that they no longer know what they truly want, or what their real feelings or interests are. In this state, they are easy prey to self–deception, deceit, and falseness of all kinds. Thus, the deeper problem is that their search for a way to be of value increasingly takes them further away from their own Essential Self with its core of real value. From their earliest years, as Threes become dependent on receiving attention from others and in pursuing the values that others reward, they gradually lose touch with themselves. Step by step, their own inner core, their “heart’s desire,” is left behind until they no longer recognize it.

Thus, while they are the primary type in the Feeling Center, Threes, interestingly, are not known as “feeling” people; rather, they are people of action and achievement. It is as if they “put their feelings in a box” so that they can get ahead with what they want to achieve. Threes have come to believe that emotions get in the way of their performance, so they substitute thinking and practical action for feelings.

Threes report that when they realize to what extent they have adapted their lives to the expectations of others, the question arises, “Well, then, what do I want?” They often simply did not know; it was not a question that had ever come up before. Thus, the fundamental dilemma of Threes is that they have not been allowed to be who they really are and to manifest their own authentic qualities. At a young age, they got the message that they were not allowed to have feelings and be themselves: they must, in effect, be someone else to be accepted. To some degree, all of the personality types have been sent the same message, but because of their particular background and makeup, Threes not only heard it, they began to live by it. The attention they received by performing in a certain way was their oxygen, and they needed it to breathe. Unfortunately, it came at a high price.
